The project aims to explore the political and social dynamics that shaped the territory of south-eastern Sicily between the 9th and 6th centuries BCE, a period that remains poorly understood yet is crucial for the island’s history. During these centuries, this area was inhabited by communities of diverse origins and represented a key node within the island’s and the wider ancient Mediterranean networks of mobility and exchange. The research seeks to move beyond the traditional narrative of relations between local and Greek populations by emphasizing the complex social, cultural, and economic networks and the significant processes of transformation affecting the territory within the selected chronological span. One of the project’s central aspects is the analysis of relational networks among settlements and populations, with particular attention to the circulation of natural resources (raw materials such as timber, metals, and stone) as well as technologies and knowledge. Tracing and visualizing these connections will help reconstruct the complexity of interactions and cultural transformations that took place in the region. Building on the rich body of archaeological research conducted over recent decades and on the availability of modern digital collections, the project aims to offer a new perspective on the past of south-eastern Sicily through an integrated methodology and a detailed digital mapping of the territory. The expertise developed in the study of the area, combined with the application of advanced digital humanities tools, will enable the collection and analysis of archaeological, epigraphic, historical, and environmental data, making them available through a dynamic and queryable digital map. This approach will allow for a better understanding of the political and social organization of local communities and of the transformations triggered by interaction with the earliest Greek poleis.
